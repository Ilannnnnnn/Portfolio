Automatic sleep apnea detection

Development of a deep learning pipeline for apnea detection from ECG, SCG and BCG signals — research internship at Erasme Hospital, Brussels.

M1 Internship · Erasme Hospital / ULB · LPHYS Laboratory · May – September 2025 · Brussels

Context

Obstructive sleep apnea-hypopnea syndrome (OSAHS) is a widespread and largely underdiagnosed disorder, characterised by repeated interruptions of breathing during sleep. These episodes cause sleep fragmentation and drops in oxygen saturation, which can lead over time to hypertension, cardiovascular disease, and metabolic disorders such as type 2 diabetes.

Polysomnography (PSG) remains the gold standard for diagnosing these conditions, but its cost, logistical complexity and the requirement for a dedicated sleep laboratory limit access. The long-term objective of this project is to develop an automatic, explainable detection system based on non-invasive signals: electrocardiogram (ECG), seismocardiography (SCG) and ballistocardiography (BCG).

This work is part of a broader research initiative between Erasme Hospital and the European Space Agency (ESA), which is developing physiological monitoring systems for extreme environments — polar stations, space missions — where PSG cannot be deployed.

Host institution

The internship took place within the Laboratory of Physics and Physiology (LPHYS), a research unit integrated into Erasme Hospital and affiliated with the Université Libre de Bruxelles. The laboratory works on kinocardiography, multimodal fusion of cardiac signals (ECG, SCG, BCG) and video-based physiological monitoring. It is funded in part by BELSPO (Belgian Science Policy Office) and ESA.

I worked under the supervision of Paniz Balali (PhD candidate) and Dr Amin Hossein, with occasional input from Professor Olivier Debeir (ULB) on AI aspects, and from Damien Gorlier on industrialisation of the Python pipeline.

Technical pipeline

The entire pipeline was developed in Python with a modular architecture, designed to be reusable, testable in isolation, and extensible to SCG and BCG signals.

ECG preprocessing

Two processing paths were designed: one for public PhysioNet data (well-structured research data) and one for Erasme Hospital clinical data (ambulatory recordings with motion artefacts, noise, clock offsets). Both paths converge to a common representation before the time-frequency transform.

  • 0.5 Hz high-pass and 50 Hz notch filter (zero-phase IIR) for clinical data
  • 5–15 Hz bandpass filter (Butterworth) centred on QRS energy
  • R-peak detection via Pan-Tompkins-style algorithm
  • Resampling to 200 Hz and segmentation into 1-minute windows aligned with PSG
  • Labelling: a segment is labelled as apnea if the total apnea duration within the minute exceeds 5 seconds

Scalogram generation

Each ECG segment is converted into a time-frequency image (scalogram) via the Continuous Wavelet Transform (CWT) with an analytic Morlet wavelet, across 128 log-spaced pseudo-frequencies in the 5–15 Hz band. Magnitudes are normalised, converted to RGB with a fixed colormap, and exported as 224 × 224 × 3 images — compatible with ImageNet-pretrained CNNs.

Model: GoogLeNet + Transfer Learning

The selected model was chosen following a comparative analysis presented to Professor Debeir. GoogLeNet, pre-trained on ImageNet, is fine-tuned for binary classification (apnea / normal). Its Inception modules enable multi-scale feature extraction with high parameter efficiency — a key advantage on limited-size biomedical datasets.

  • Two optimiser groups: classification heads learn 10x faster than the backbone
  • Cross-entropy loss with auxiliary losses weighted at 0.3 (Inception architecture)
  • Mixed precision (AMP) on GPU, dropout p = 0.5
  • 5-fold cross-validation, strictly patient-wise (no patient overlaps between subsets)
  • Progressive unfreezing: FT-4 (from Inception 4a) or FT-3 (from Inception 3a)

All validation decisions (thresholds, model selection) are made on the validation set only. The test set remains strictly unseen until final evaluation — unlike the practice observed in the reference paper, which I documented and flagged to the authors.

Results & Key takeaways

Four configurations were evaluated under a strict patient-wise protocol, with metrics computed per fold and aggregated.

Configuration Accuracy F1 Recall Specificity MCC AUC
PhysioNet → PhysioNet (in-domain) 0.89 0.83 0.85 0.90 0.75 0.94
PhysioNet → Erasme (no adaptation) 0.63 0.18 0.15 0.83 -0.01 0.49
Erasme → Erasme (in-domain) 0.69 0.16 0.12 0.93 0.08 0.59
PhysioNet + Fine-tuning → Erasme 0.68 0.30 0.26 0.85 0.13 0.65

In-domain results on PhysioNet are stable and credible under strict patient-wise evaluation. Direct transfer to Erasme collapses, confirming a strong domain shift driven by motion artefacts, cohort differences and acquisition context. Targeted fine-tuning meaningfully improves recall and AUC on Erasme without modifying the scalogram representation.

Challenges

Challenge 01 · Data
PSG annotation labelling and consistency
PSG exports contained clinically incompatible overlapping events (e.g. simultaneous obstructive and central apneas). After liaising with the PSG team, I corrected the extraction scripts, regenerated the event tables, and added quality controls: mutual exclusivity, mass verification (sum of apnea seconds per minute = total annotated ± tolerance), and traceability archiving.
Challenge 02 · Temporal alignment
Movesense / PSG synchronisation — up to 5-hour offset
Movesense and PSG clocks were not synchronised. The mean offset discovered was ~2 hours, with a maximum of 5 hours — well beyond the initial assumption of a few minutes. Root causes: PSG encoded in GMT vs. local Brussels time (CET/CEST), and daylight saving time changes during the recording period. Solution: UTC normalisation, raw offset estimation via RR series cross-correlation, then beat-by-beat refinement.
Challenge 03 · Reproducibility
Methodological limitations in the reference paper
Attempting to reproduce Choudhury et al. (2025) revealed several inconsistencies: dropout reported as 1–7% but displayed as 10–70% in figures, hyperparameters selected on the test set (data leakage), segment-level rather than patient-level splits. Direct correspondence with the first author confirmed. These findings shaped my methodological choices towards a strictly conservative evaluation framework.
Challenge 04 · Infrastructure
Instability of the shared Einstein machine
For approximately three weeks, long training runs could not complete reliably on the shared Einstein workstation (GPU conflicts, disk saturation, version incompatibilities). Migrated to ULB's LISA servers with pinned environments, timestamped run folders, and deterministic checkpoint-resume scripts.
Challenge 05 · Tooling
Simultaneous adoption of Click, just and uv
Introducing three new tools at once (Click CLI framework, just task runner, uv environment manager) while cleaning data and training models created significant cognitive load. Resolved by stabilising the CLIs first, keeping the justfile minimal, and centralising defaults in a single Python module imported everywhere.

Future directions

The ECG pipeline is delivered with SCG/BCG integration hooks, frozen CSV splits and patient-wise evaluation. Next steps include:

  • Multimodal fusion: activating SCG and BCG branches, starting with late fusion (one CNN branch per modality + embedding concatenation), then systematic ablations — ECG alone vs ECG+SCG vs ECG+BCG vs all
  • Decision threshold calibration per fold on the validation set only (Youden criterion or F1-max), then fixed for the final test
  • Class imbalance handling: replacing subsampling with a weighted loss or focal loss to retain more data from the small Erasme dataset
  • Explainability: integrating LIME analyses once the operating point is fixed, to validate that model decisions correspond to genuine physiological patterns
  • Apnea sub-type differentiation (obstructive vs central) once multimodal fusion is in place
