GWARM

Predicting enzyme thermostability by fine-tuning a protein language model — towards AI-assisted engineering of hyperthermophilic enzymes.

Context

Research project conducted during the first year of the Master's programme at ISEN Brest (January to April 2025), under the supervision of Matthieu Saumard and Yann Moalic, researchers specialising in bioinformatics.

Hyperthermophilic enzymes — capable of functioning above 80°C — are a valuable resource for biotechnology industries: biofuels, green chemistry, and organic waste degradation. The challenge: metabolic pathways of interest are often absent in hyperthermophilic organisms, and simply transferring genes from mesophilic organisms fails. The translated proteins prove non-functional at high temperatures, due to deep differences in genetic code, mRNA structure and regulatory signals.

GWARM explores a radically different approach: leveraging hundreds of millions of known protein sequences to train an AI model capable of predicting whether an enzyme is thermostable — and, in the longer term, of rewriting mesophilic sequences to make them compatible with hyperthermophilic conditions.

Dataset construction

One of the project's major contributions is the assembly of an original large-scale biological dataset, fully automated.

Organism selection

Two databases were used: GOSHA (Zhang et al., 2023), which lists over 18,000 organisms with validated optimal growth temperatures, and ThermoBase (Li et al., 2021), specialised in thermophilic species. A Python script filtering by temperature threshold (≤ 38°C for mesophiles, ≥ 50°C for thermophiles) generated a base of ~24,000 mesophiles and ~1,200 thermophiles.

Genome retrieval and processing

Genomes were retrieved via the NCBI Entrez API, with rate limiting (max 3/s) and retry on error. The .faa files containing protein sequences were downloaded in parallel, totalling approximately 25 GB of data.

Homologous pair construction

BLASTP identified homologous pairs between mesophilic and thermophilic organisms (≥ 50% identity, E-value ≤ 1e-10, bit score ≥ 50). Calculations parallelised across 6 cores: approximately 1 million representative pairs from 42+ million raw sequences.

Model architecture

Three candidates were evaluated before selecting ESM-2.

Alternative evaluation

  • Evo 2 — Foundation genomic model (9.3 trillion base pairs), oriented towards DNA and generation, not protein classification. Too computationally expensive.
  • ProtBert — BERT architecture adapted to proteins, 420M parameters. Solid, but less flexible than ESM-2.
  • ESM-2 (selected) — Meta AI, trained on UniRef50 (250M+ sequences), native PyTorch integration, state-of-the-art performance.

ESM-2 implementation

Variant selected: facebook/esm2_t6_8M_UR50D — 6 Transformer layers, 8M parameters. Fine-tuned as an AutoModelForSequenceClassification for binary prediction (thermophile / mesophile).

ESM-2 embeddings implicitly encode structural information (secondary and tertiary structure, functional domains, active sites) from amino acid sequence alone — without multiple sequence alignment or prior annotation.

Training pipeline

  • Sequence tokenisation (max 512 tokens, per amino-acid strategy)
  • Stratified 80% / 10% / 10% split (train / val / test)
  • Adam optimiser, learning rate 1e-4, batch size 256
  • Early stopping on validation loss
  • Training on Google Colab (GPU T4/V100), ~1 hour

AUC of 0.66 should be interpreted within the training constraints (fewer than 10,000 steps). Tests at 110,000 steps and 1,024 tokens produced an accuracy of 0.9994 and an F1 of 0.9994 — performance limited by resources, not architecture.

The confusion matrix reveals an asymmetry: 52 thermophiles misclassified as mesophiles (false negatives), versus only one mesophile predicted as thermophilic. This reflects the greater structural diversity of mesophilic enzymes in the dataset.

Key takeaways

  • End-to-end computational bioinformatics pipeline: from raw genomic collection to deep learning training
  • Exploration of Protein Language Models (ESM-2, ProtBert, Evo 2) for functional classification
  • Management of large-scale datasets (25 GB, 42M sequences) with parallelisation and API error handling
  • Direct encounter with the computational limits of academic research and the impact of training steps
  • Interdisciplinary work at the intersection of synthetic biology, comparative genomics and applied machine learning

Results obtained under constrained conditions outperform several references in the thermostability prediction literature. This project lays the groundwork for in silico enzyme engineering — faster and more cost-effective than traditional directed evolution approaches.

Future directions

  • Regression task: predicting optimal temperature on a continuous scale
  • Full fine-tuning: 50,000+ steps with 1,024-token sequences on distributed infrastructure
  • Generative engineering: combining ESM-2 as a discriminator with Evo 2 to generate thermostabilising mutations
  • Accessible interface: Streamlit or Gradio deployment for biologists without programming skills
  • Scientific publication: results targeting ISMB/ECCB or Bioinformatics
